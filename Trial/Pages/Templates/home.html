Coal has powered industries and economies for centuries, but its environmental cost is undeniable. Coal mining operations release massive amounts of greenhouse gases, including carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄), both of which trap heat and accelerate global warming.

India, as the second-largest coal producer in the world, depends heavily on coal for energy. However, this comes with severe environmental consequences, including rising temperatures, extreme weather, deforestation, and air pollution.

Without accurate tracking and reduction of emissions, the negative impacts of coal mining will continue to escalate.

How Coal Mining Contributes to Global Warming

1. Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

The Leading Cause of Global Temperature Rise

Coal mining emits significant amounts of CO₂, primarily from:

  • Diesel-powered equipment used in excavation, transportation, and drilling.
  • Blasting operations, which release CO₂ from explosives.
  • Fossil fuel-based electricity used in underground mine ventilation and processing plants.
  • Transportation of coal, which relies on high-emission trucks, railways, and conveyor belts.

Once released into the atmosphere, CO₂ can persist for hundreds of years, continuously trapping heat. The energy sector, largely driven by coal, is responsible for nearly 40% of global CO₂ emissions.

2. Methane (CH₄)

A Greenhouse Gas 80 Times More Potent than CO₂

Coal mining is a major source of methane (CH₄), a gas far more effective at trapping heat than CO₂. Methane is released from:

  • Coal seams during mining operations.
  • Ventilation systems in underground mines, which pump methane-laden air into the atmosphere.
  • Abandoned coal mines, which continue to leak methane long after mining stops.

While methane remains in the atmosphere for a shorter duration than CO₂, its heat-trapping ability is 80 times stronger over a 20-year period.

3. Air Pollution and Extreme Weather

Health Risks and Environmental Damage

Coal mining releases fine particulate matter (PM2.5), sulfur dioxide (SO₂), and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), all of which contribute to severe air pollution and respiratory diseases.

  • Increased smog and poor air quality lead to lung diseases and heart conditions.
  • Sulfur emissions contribute to acid rain, destroying crops, forests, and aquatic ecosystems.
  • Climate change leads to unpredictable weather patterns, including heatwaves and floods.

Rising temperatures and extreme weather events threaten both human lives and ecosystems globally.

4. Deforestation and Land Degradation

Loss of Natural Carbon Sinks

Coal mining, particularly open-cast mining, leads to:

  • Massive deforestation, reducing Earth's ability to absorb CO₂ naturally.
  • Loss of biodiversity, as habitats are destroyed for mining infrastructure.
  • Soil erosion and degradation, making land unsuitable for agriculture.

The loss of forests eliminates natural carbon sinks and worsens local climate conditions.

The Urgent Need for Action

With climate change worsening, the role of coal mining in global emissions must be addressed. However, many mining companies lack accurate tools to quantify their emissions, making it difficult to track progress and implement solutions.

Shortcomings in Current Emission Calculation Models

Current carbon footprint calculation models for coal mines suffer from several significant limitations that prevent accurate measurement and meaningful reduction of emissions:

Static Emission Factors

Existing models rely on generic emission factors that fail to account for regional variations in coal properties, mine-specific conditions, and operational differences.

Incomplete Emission Sources

Most models overlook critical sources like post-mining methane leakage, Scope 3 emissions from transportation and equipment, and fugitive methane from underground operations.

Mine-Type Variability

The distinct emission profiles of open-cast mines versus underground mines are rarely differentiated in current tools.

Methodology Discrepancies

"Bottom-up" estimates often exceed "top-down" measurements by 10–24%, largely due to inadequate accounting for non-combustion coal uses.

Lack of Real-Time Data

Traditional models rely on annual production figures rather than continuous monitoring, resulting in delayed and potentially inaccurate emissions reporting.

Regional Blind Spots

India-specific factors like high-ash coal content and shallow mining depths increase emissions but are ignored by global models.
